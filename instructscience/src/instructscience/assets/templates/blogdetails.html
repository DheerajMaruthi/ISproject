With self-quarantining taking a toll on individuals and businesses around the world, many are eagerly waiting for life to get back to as normal as things were before the virus started. The outbreak of COVID-19 has proven things that were once considered impossible are now being practiced excessively but with some challenges and caveats. With several countries gradually easing their isolation restrictions, the exercise of working from home is being shifted back to working from conventional offices with additional precautionary measures. This involves the transformation of traditional workplaces into an environment that is safe, hygienic, and caters to mental health needs. In order to make your workplace more safe and healthy, it is important to adhere to the guidelines essayed by organizations such as national Center for Disease Control (CDC), The American Society of Heating, Refrigeration and Air-conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE), and Indian Society of Heating, Refrigerating & Air (ISHRAE) to control transmission of airborne particles and ensure a safe, healthy workplace. Here are five reasons why its important to increase ventilation in buildings: -

  • Since COVID-19 is an airborne virus, diluting the pathogens inside the building can reduce the risk of its transmission. It is, therefore, recommended to ensure good airflow and ventilation rates through the installation of an air quality control system.
  • Trapping of stale air enhances the risk of virus indoors. Hence, high priority should be given to a well-designed and effective ventilation system when it comes to building a space that ensures the flow of clean and fresh air.
  • The risks of getting infected with COVID-19 can increase in winters as it is a season when we spend most of our time indoors. Hence, in order to curtail the spread of infection, it is vital to consider indoor air quality diagnostics and ventilate the building effectively.
  • A buildings ventilation system should be checked at regular intervals to ensure there is enough airflow to dilute the virus in the inside air. One way to fulfil this is by ventilating the building for long or opting for ventilation with higher airflow volumes. 
  • Poor indoor air quality is not only responsible for increasing the risk of COVID-19 but also causes various respiratory problems. A joint report by the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health (RCPCH) and the Royal College of Physicians show that there is growing evidence that respiratory issues among children may be outcomes of bad air quality in homes, schools, or nurseries. This landmark report, involving a systematic review of 221 studies, present evidences that number of child health problems including asthma, conjunctivitis, wheezing, dermatitis, and eczema are the result of poor indoor air quality. Within the report, the sources of indoor air pollution are cited as smoking, dampness, burning of fossil fuels, the presence of dust particles, chemicals from building materials, furnishings, and so on. 

     

Ventilation guidelines in COVID

In order to ensure the flow of clean air and maintain the thermal health of buildings, the Indian Society of Heating, Refrigerating, and Air Conditioning has come up with some guidelines that need to be followed at homes, offices, and hospitals. These guidelines include: 

  • Ensuring the thermal comfort of buildings by insulating the building properly
  • Maintaining humidity levels between 40% and 60%
  • Providing a high rate of airflow
  • Reducing the building’s temperature through passive design upgrades

According to professionals and experts, terminating the use of air conditioners to prevent the spread of COVID-19 infection is not a long-term solution, and improving the ventilation system should be emphasized more. Most offices don’t prefer air conditioners that allow the intake of fresh air as they increase the cost of running. However, in the current circumstances, clean and fresh air is one of the most important things that we need. As per experts, air conditioners should be run at 24 – 27 degrees to maintain the thermal health of buildings. Moreover, the air ducts of the ventilation system should also be cleaned at regular intervals to make sure that clean air circulates inside the building. 

What should the new and existing buildings do to ensure adequate ventilation?

As the COVID-19 infection spreads at a rapid pace, new buildings should invest more in passive design upgrades and retrofits that can help in improving shading, insulation, and ventilation. New buildings should also keep ventilation for each room in primary focus along with the increased usage of technologies like H-13 certified filters, capable of processing particles up to 0.003 microns. In order to improve the thermal health of buildings, the overall heat load needs to be reduced. This can be done through the installation of rooftop solar panels or vertical gardens. In the case of older buildings devoid of any heating or ventilation systems, they should simply open windows to let in the outdoor air.

It is therefore,  important to realize that while recirculating air has become the default in our buildings, ventilating with outdoor air is vital in diluting the airborne contaminants and decreasing the transmission of infection. Hence, it wouldn’t be wrong to say that in addition to everyday precautions taken by individuals, the building owners, managers, and employers have a vital role to play in creating a safe environment for themselves and other inhabitants.
